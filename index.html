Holdspace uses minimal textual information and focuses on a single dynamic visual interaction instead, which is a 3d pentagon made of black outlines, that changes size and movement in response to sound. There's almost no instructions, which is to intentionally leave users to intuitively explore the relationship between sound and form. The overall UI is extremely minimalistic, using only black and white to contrast the background and guide the user to focus on different things. When the user is playing the music, there is a white background and the pentagon is specifically outlined in black, to draw the user’s attention immediately towards it. This whitespace amplifies the movement of the pentagon, making it become more dramatic and noticeable because there’s nothing competing with it. However, when the user stops, the background suddenly turns black, to mimic being sucked in. Since there is immediate visual contrast from the white and a pointy triangle resembling a shard is left in its stead, it makes the user feel more unwelcomed. This encourages the user to continue their experience of the pentagon. Similarly, in Liquid entropy the entire page feels alive due to movement and sound, not text or buttons, making the aim of both websites to evoke a feeling from users. Since its visuals create a floating, ambient effect, the overall mood is dominant. However, Liquid entropy takes a very different approach towards whitespace where it’s less structured and relies more on atmosphere. Rather than large blank areas, the space is often filled with ambient visuals and floating elements, making it feel occupied all the time. There is also no central focus, like Holdspace and the whitespace is empty, which is why the users are left in ambiguity.

Mapping

Holdspace is very direct in its mapping since it only has one control, which is the space bar. There are no visible UI elements like buttons or sliders, since the shape and sound act as the real time feedback. Despite having only one input, the control manages multiple simultaneous variables such as shape, sound, motion, and size, which makes the experience very expressive and encourages immersion. However, Liquid entropy uses more traditional UI components such as sliders which allows users to adjust different controls to manipulate their audiovisual experience. Since every slider allows for the adjustment of different sensory variables, it helps make the mapping indirect. While the controls are clear, the system’s response can feel more ambiguous or interpretive which invites exploration and is designed to let users play however they like. Overall, Holdspace shows how a single, familiar control can be mapped to a multisensory experience, giving a fixed response. However, Liquid entropy uses traditional UI components and creates a space where users shape emotional and perceptual tone themselves rather than manipulating fixed outcomes. Despite their differing mappings, both websites succeed in communicating through interaction through careful synchronisation of audio, visuals, and motion.

Characterisation

In Holdspace, characterisation emerges from the users familiarity with the beat, which causes rhythmic expansion, making the pentagon feel alive. This experience is caused by how fast the pentagon is moving and how it grows or shrinks based on volume, mimicking a pulse. This rhythmic pace creates a sense of spontaneity while subtly training users to associate volume and sound with movement. The tumbling and rumbling of the pentagon aligns with the beat, reinforcing users’ expectations that the shape will grow on impact, further enhancing the feeling of movement in response to sound. When the user lets go of the spacebar, the pentagon collapses into a triangle, the music becomes quieter and higher-pitched, and movement ceases which clearly signals a change in state which allows the users to quickly learn that when the pentagon appears, they are in control and when the triangle appears, they are not, which sets this expectation. Similarly, Liquid entropy also relies on the familiarity that users have in movement and sound. However, the website does this by having a more continuous interaction where the visuals follow the cursor so it really feels like the sound is following them. Every time there is an upwards motion, the website responds with a higher pitch and the cursor follows the visuals, while a downward motion lowers the pitch and retreats the visuals. Overall, Holdspace feels like a rhythmic companion whilst Liquid entropy feels more malleable, constantly adapting to the user’s movements.

Feedback

In Holdspace , the feedback is binary and reactive; by letting the spacebar go, it instantly transforms the visual form from a still triangle to a dynamic, pulsing pentagon. This visual shift is paired with a change in audio where the beat becomes louder and more rhythmic, creating an immediate cause and effect relationship. The presence of a timer at the top also reinforces the idea of control, subtly guiding user behavior through visual cues. In contrast, Liquid entropy offers a more continuous, ambient feedback where users receive real time auditory and visual responses through sliders and cursor movement. As the user moves the cursor, the pitch and visuals adjust, which results in feedback that feels fluid rather than reactive. However, both websites have immediate feedback which is comforting for the user.
